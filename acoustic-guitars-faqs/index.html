Acoustic Guitars questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A dreadnought is the most popular acoustic guitar shape for its loud, balanced tone and comfortable body, making it a versatile choice for beginners. The best beginner guitar is one that feels comfortable in your hands, stays in tune, and fits your budget, typically a well-built dreadnought from a reliable brand starting at a few hundred dollars. Focus on playability, build quality, and a proper setup rather than chasing specs or brand hype.

What is a dreadnought acoustic guitar, and why is it so popular?

A dreadnought is a large-bodied acoustic guitar with a pronounced waist and broad lower bout, originally designed by Martin in the early 20th century to produce louder, fuller sound than parlor or concert shapes. Its deep bass and strong projection make it a favorite for strummers and flatpickers across folk, rock, country, and pop. The dreadnought’s popularity comes from its ability to hold up in a mix, it cuts through without sounding thin. For beginners, its size can feel a bit bulky if you have a smaller frame, but many players adapt quickly. Most dreadnoughts have a scale length around 25 to 26 inches, which gives strings a bit more tension and a punchy attack.

How do I choose the right size and shape for my body?

Body size affects both sound and comfort. Dreadnoughts are on the larger end; if you are small-statured or have shorter arms, a dreadnought might feel cumbersome, especially when sitting. In that case, look at “mini” or “travel” dreadnoughts, full-size brands offer these with slightly reduced depth or shorter scale, retaining a lot of the dreadnought character in a more manageable package. Zero in on the guitars that feel natural in your hands. Sit with the guitar in a store, rest it on your right leg, and see if your strumming arm lands comfortably without reaching. Concert and grand auditorium shapes are narrower and often easier for beginners. The key is to try before you buy or check return policies on online orders.

What wood types matter most for tone and budget?

The top wood is the most important: solid spruce is the gold standard for dreadnoughts, it’s responsive, bright, and ages well. Cedar and mahogany tops offer warmer, darker tones but are less common on entry-level dreadnoughts. Many affordable models use a solid spruce top with laminated back and sides, which saves cost while keeping decent sound quality. Back and side woods affect the character: mahogany gives a focused, woody midrange; rosewood adds shimmering overtones and bass bloom; sapele and nato are budget-friendly alternatives that lean toward mahogany-like tones. Beginners should prioritize a solid top over expensive back/side wood. It’s a common upgrade that improves projection and dynamic range.

How much should a beginner spend on their first acoustic?

Expect to spend in the “budget-friendly” to “a few hundred dollars” range for a guitar that won’t fight you. At the lower end, you can find decent players from trusted brands, though you may encounter higher action and cheaper hardware. Stepping up by about 50–100% often gets you a solid top, better tuners, and a more consistent setup out of the box. It’s not about the price tag alone, a well-made budget dreadnought from Yamaha, Fender, or Ibanez can serve you for years. Avoid the cheapest “no-name” guitars sold in big-box stores; they often warp and frustrate beginners. A used model from a reputable maker can also be a smart buy, if you have someone experienced to inspect it.

What features make a guitar easier to play for a beginner?

A low “action”. the height of the strings above the frets, is the single biggest factor. Factory setups often leave the action high, causing sore fingers and buzzing. Ask a guitar shop to do a setup (or buy from one that does it free). A comfortable neck shape and narrow nut width (around 1-11/16 inches) also help small hands get clean chords. Light-gauge strings (like.011–.052) are easier on beginner fingertips but still sound full on a dreadnought. A shorter scale length (under 25.5 inches) reduces string tension, making bends and barre chords more manageable. Many student-oriented dreadnoughts offer shorter scales. Also consider a cutaway if you plan to play above the 12th fret, it adds access without a major tone trade-off.

What are the most common mistakes beginners make when buying?

Buying purely on looks or a low price is the biggest pitfall. A glossy, sunburst guitar with terrible action will discourage practice. Another mistake: ignoring the importance of a setup. Even a good guitar can feel unplayable if the neck relief or saddle height is off. Many beginners also overestimate the dreadnought’s size, if you can’t comfortably wrap your arm around the body, consider a smaller shape. Don’t mistake brand cachet for quality. A well-reviewed budget model from a major brand often beats an entry-level “pro” model from a luxury name. Finally, avoid buying the first guitar you touch. Play a few in your price range, or at least listen to demo videos with headphones to hear the tonal differences.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a dreadnought and a concert guitar?

A dreadnought is larger with a wider waist and deeper body, producing louder bass and more volume. A concert guitar is smaller, lighter, and more balanced across frequencies, with a slightly sweeter midrange, easier to hold but less powerful for strumming.

Is a solid top better than a laminated top?

Yes, a solid top is almost always preferable because it vibrates more freely, improving resonance and projection. Laminated tops are cheaper and more humidity-proof, but they sound less dynamic. For a first guitar, a solid spruce top is a worthy upgrade if you can stretch your budget.

Do I need a cutaway as a beginner?

Not necessarily. A cutaway gives easier access to high frets, but most beginners rarely go above the 12th fret in the first year. Non-cutaway dreadnoughts often have a fuller bass response and are slightly cheaper. If you plan to play lead lines or want flexibility, a cutaway is a nice option.

What gauge strings should I start with?

Light gauge (typically.011–.052.012–.053) is ideal for beginners, they require less finger strength and produce a bright, responsive sound. Extra-light sets can be even easier, but may sound thin on a dreadnought. Heavier strings (like.013–.056) are harder on the fingers and better suited to experienced players.

How important is a setup from the store?

Extremely important. A proper setup adjusts the neck relief, nut slots, saddle height, and intonation, all of which affect playability and tone. Many budget guitars come with action set too high. If the store offers a free setup with purchase, take it. If not, budget – for a professional setup elsewhere.

Can I learn on a cheap guitar?

Yes, if it’s from a reputable brand and has been properly set up. A cheap guitar that frets cleanly, stays in tune, and feels comfortable is perfectly fine to start. The danger is buying an ultra-low-cost guitar that fights you at every chord, that’s a motivation killer. Look for known entry-level lines like Yamaha’s F-series or Fender’s CD series.

In shortYour first acoustic guitar should invite you to play every day. Focus on comfort, a solid top if possible, and a dreadnought (or a smaller alternative) that feels right in your hands. A modest, well-set-up model from a trusted maker will teach you more than a flashy, expensive one that sits in its case. Take your time, try before you buy, and don’t overlook a professional setup, it transforms how a guitar plays. Happy strumming.
