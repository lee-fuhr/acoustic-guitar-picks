How to choose a acoustic guitars

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose an acoustic guitar as a beginner, focus on a dreadnought body style for its strong, balanced sound and comfortable size. Look for a solid spruce top over laminate for better resonance, and prioritize playability with a low action and a neck shape that fits your hand. Stick with trusted brands like Yamaha, Fender, or Epiphone in the budget-friendly to mid-range price category.

What size acoustic guitar should a beginner choose?

If you are new to guitar and want a versatile, loud, and full-sounding instrument, a dreadnought is your best bet. It strikes a smart balance: not as bulky as a jumbo but larger than a concert or parlor model, so it delivers punchy lows and clear highs without overwhelming your lap. Most dreadnoughts have a lower bout around 15–16 inches wide and a depth of 4–5 inches, which feels substantial but still manageable for most adults. Smaller body sizes like the concert or auditorium are easier to hold if you have a shorter torso or smaller hands, but they produce less volume and bass. For a first guitar, dreadnought gives you the most “acoustic guitar” sound per dollar. If you are a younger player or have a slight build, try holding a dreadnought before buying, some find it a stretch, but others love the fullness.

What wood types matter for sound and durability?

On a beginner dreadnought, the top wood has the biggest impact on tone. Solid spruce is the industry standard: it is bright, articulate, and projects well. Many affordable dreadnoughts use a solid spruce top paired with laminated mahogany or sapele back and sides. This keeps costs down while still giving you a responsive sound that improves as you play it in. Laminate woods, thin layers glued together, are more resistant to humidity and temperature changes, making them ideal for beginners in variable climates. But they sound a bit less lively than all-solid guitars. If your budget allows for a solid-top model, go for it. You will get better resonance and a guitar that ages well. Common back-and-side woods like mahogany produce a warm, focused tone, while rosewood offers deeper bass and shimmering trebles, though rosewood is typically found at a slightly higher price point.

Should beginners buy an all-solid or laminate guitar?

For most beginners, a guitar with a solid top and laminate body is the sweet spot. It keeps the cost very reasonable, often within a few hundred dollars, while giving you the tonal benefits of a solid soundboard. Laminate sides and back are durable and help tame unwanted overtones, which can actually make a beginner guitar easier to record or play with others. All-solid guitars offer richer, more complex tone and greater projection, but they are more expensive and require careful humidity control. As a first instrument, you do not need that level of complexity. Stick with a reputable solid-top dreadnought from a major brand. You can always upgrade to an all-solid model later, once you know you will keep playing and have a stable environment for it.

What features affect playability for new players?

Playability is the most important factor for a beginner. Look for a guitar with a low action, the height of the strings above the fretboard. A comfortable action makes pressing down strings easier and reduces finger pain. Most budget-friendly dreadnoughts come with a nut width around 1.69 to 1.75 inches; that is wide enough for fingerstyle but not so wide that barre chords become a struggle. The neck shape also matters. Modern profiles, like a slim “C” or “soft V,” feel more ergonomic than chunky vintage shapes. If possible, visit a store and grip a few necks. And do not overlook the importance of proper string gauge. Many beginner guitars ship with medium strings (like.012–.053), which sound full but can be tough on tender fingers. Lighter gauge strings (such as.011.010) are gentler while still sounding good on a dreadnought.

What is a reasonable budget for a beginner dreadnought?

You do not need to spend a huge amount to get a reliable, good-sounding dreadnought. The most honest advice is to plan on spending in the lower-to-mid range of what the market offers for a solid-top model. Avoid the very cheapest instruments (often under a certain threshold), because they usually have poor build quality, high action, and cheap hardware that will frustrate you. A good target is about one to three hundred dollars, depending on where you shop. At that price, you can find dreadnoughts from Yamaha, Fender, and Epiphone that are consistent and playable. If you can stretch a bit higher, say, to the mid-range, you get better tone woods, better tuners, and a setup that is more ready out of the box. Remember, a slightly more expensive guitar that you love playing is a bargain compared to a cheap one that collects dust.

Which well-known brands offer reliable beginner dreadnoughts?

Yamaha is the gold standard for beginner acoustic guitars. Their dreadnought models, like the FG series, are known for solid construction, good intonation, and a warm, balanced tone, often exceeding their price tag. Fender’s CD-60 is another consistent choice, offering a comfortable neck and versatile sound. Epiphone’s dreadnoughts (such as the DR-100) are budget-friendly and have a classic look and feel. Other names worth considering include Ibanez (with their PF series), Recording King (great value in all-solid entry models), and Takamine (known for durability and built-in electronics on some models). Avoid no-name brands that cut corners on bracing and fretwork. Sticking with one of these established brands gives you a guitar that will stay playable for years, with good resale value if you decide to upgrade.

Frequently asked questions

Is a dreadnought too big for a beginner?

Not for most adult players. The dreadnought body is large enough to create powerful bass and volume, yet still comfortable when seated. If you are very small or have a short reach, you might prefer a concert or 3/4-size guitar. But many beginners find the dreadnought fine once they get used to it.

How often should I change the strings on a beginner dreadnought?

Change strings every one to three months, depending on how often you play and whether your hands sweat heavily. Dull, rusty strings sound lifeless and are harder to fret. For a new player, fresh strings can make a big difference in motivation and tone.

Should I buy a beginner pack that includes a gig bag, tuner, and picks?

Beginner packs can be a good value if the guitar itself is from a reputable brand. They often save you money on accessories. Just check that the included tuner is accurate and the bag is padded. You can also buy a quality guitar separately and pick your own accessories later.

What is the difference between an acoustic guitar and a classical guitar?

Classical guitars have nylon strings, softer on the fingers, and a wider, flatter neck. They are great for fingerstyle and flamenco music. Dreadnought acoustics use steel strings, which are brighter and louder, making them better for strumming and folk/rock genres. Beginners should choose based on the music they want to play.

Do I need a cutaway on a dreadnought?

A cutaway (the notch at the upper bout) gives you access to the highest frets, which is useful if you plan to play lead lines high on the neck. For most beginners who are just strumming chords in open positions, a cutaway is not necessary and can add a little cost. A standard dreadnought is perfectly fine.

How important is a built-in tuner or pickup for a beginner?

Not very important. A clip-on tuner costs very little and is more accurate than many built-in tuners. Pickups are only useful if you plan to plug into an amplifier or PA system. For a beginner learning at home, you are better off spending your money on guitar quality rather than electronics.

In shortChoosing your first dreadnought acoustic guitar comes down to three things: a comfortable size that feels good in your hands, a solid top for tone that will hold up over time, and a brand with a reputation for consistent quality. If you can visit a store and play a few, you will quickly feel the difference in neck shape and action. But even a well-researched online purchase from Yamaha, Fender, or Epiphone will get you started on the right foot. Take care of your guitar, keep the strings fresh, and practice regularly, that combination will turn a decent instrument into your favorite companion.
